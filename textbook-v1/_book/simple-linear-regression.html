9 Simple linear regression

9.1 Introduction

Simple linear regression (SLR) is a common statistical technique to determine how one variable \(y\) depends on another variable \(x\). The following terms are equivalent:

\(x\) \(y\)
Explanatory Response
Independent Dependent
Predictor Predicted

Given bivariate data \((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), ..., (x_n, y_n)\) and errors \(\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2, ..., \epsilon_n\), the SLR model is as follows: \[\begin{equation} y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \epsilon_i \end{equation}\]

where \[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}(Y_i \:\vert\:x_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i \quad i = 1, 2, ..., n \end{equation}\]

Here, \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) are unknown parameters. The parameter \(\beta_0\) is the ``population’’ intercept and \(\beta_1\) is the average change in \(y\) given a unit increase in \(x\). Further, \(\beta_0\), \(\beta_1\), and \(x_i\) are assumed to fixed whereas \(y_i\) are random variables. The conditional distribution of \(y_i\) given \(x_i\) is \(N(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i, \sigma^2)\). The expected value of this distribution is \[\begin{align} \mathbb{E}(y \:\vert\:x) &= \mathbb{E}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \epsilon) \\ &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \mathbb{E}(\epsilon) \\ &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 x \end{align}\]

and the variance is \[\begin{align} V(y \:\vert\:x) &= V(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \epsilon) \\ &= V(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x) + V(\epsilon) \\ &= V(\epsilon) \end{align}\]

The SLR model assumes that the distribution of \(\epsilon\) at any \(x\) has a mean of 0 (\(\mu_\epsilon = 0\)) and a constant standard deviation. The model also assumes that the distribution of \(\epsilon\) at any \(x\) is normal and the random deviations \(\epsilon_i\) are independent of one another. To fit a regression line, we must estimate \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\). That is, we want to choose \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) to minimize the sum of squared deviations in \(y\). The deviations in \(y\) can be written as follows: \[\begin{equation} f(\beta_0, \beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)^2 \end{equation}\]

9.2 Least Squares Estimation

9.2.1 Proposition

Let \(f\) be a function that represents the squared deviations in \(y\). \[\begin{equation} f(\beta_0, \beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)^2 \end{equation}\]

Then \[\begin{align} \beta_1 &= \frac{S_{XY}}{S_{XX}} \\ \beta_0 &= \bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x} \end{align}\]

are the values that minimize equation (10).

9.2.2 Proof

We first take the partial derivatives of \(f\). \[\begin{align} \frac{\partial f}{\partial \beta_0} &= \sum_{i=1}^n 2 (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) (-1) = 0 \\ 0 &= \sum_{i=1}^n (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i - y_i) \\ &= n \beta_0 + \beta_1 \sum_{i=1}^n x_i - \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ \beta_0 &= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i - \frac{\beta_1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \\ &= \bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x} \end{align}\]

Thus, \(\beta_0 = \bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x}\). \[\begin{align} \frac{\partial f}{\partial \beta_1} &= \sum_{i=1}^n 2 (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) (-x_i) = 0 \\ 0 &= \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i - \beta_0 x_i - \beta_1 x_i^2) \intertext{Substitute equation (17) into equation (19).} 0 &= \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i - (\bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x})x_i - \beta_1 x_i^2) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i - x_i \bar{y} + \beta_1 x_i \bar{x} - \beta_1 x_i^2) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - x_i \bar{y}) - \beta_1 \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i^2 - x_i \bar{x}) \\ \beta_1 &= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i - x_i \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i^2 - x_i \bar{x})} \\ &= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i) - n \bar{x} \bar{y}}{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 - n \bar{x}^2} \intertext{We can clean up equation (24) by adding the following: $\sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{x}^2 - x_i \bar{x})$ and $\sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{x}\bar{y} - y_i \bar{x})$. Note that these expressions both evaluate to 0.} \beta_1 &= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_i - x_i \bar{y}) + \sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{x} \bar{y} - \bar{x} y_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i^2 - x_i \bar{x}) + \sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{x}^2 - x_i \bar{x})} \\ &= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x}) (y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \\ &= \frac{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x}) (y_i - \bar{y})}{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \\ &= \frac{\text{Cov}(x, y)}{\text{Var}(x)} \\ &= \frac{S_{XY}}{S_{XX}} \end{align}\]
